## 3. PROPOSED KNOWLEDGE ENTRIES (deduplicated, evergreen, ready to add)

**Entry 1 - Grade the trend first (the open-reading routine)**
Category: method
Before looking for any entry, grade the trend. Read the alignment and slope of the 20, 50, and 200 SMAs (she works primarily on the 30-min, and will drop to the hourly or 4-hour when the 30-min is too noisy). Positive trend (averages rising, stacked bullishly): dips are buys. Negative trend (averages falling): bounces are sell zones. Sideways (50 flat, pivots clustered close together): trade the edges, not the middle, and wait for a breakout. Pick whichever timeframe shows the formation most cleanly rather than defaulting to a fixed one. This grade sets your bias for the whole session before a single level-touch entry is considered.

**Entry 2 - 200 SMA as mean-reversion magnet and multi-timeframe trend filter**
Category: method
The 200 SMA is the primary long-term filter and a mean-reversion target, not just a binary gate. When price drifts far above the rising 20/50/200, demand is exhausting and price tends to rotate back into the averages: that compression back toward the averages is the dip-buy setup, and watching the averages tighten is the signal it is near. Read the 200 on multiple timeframes (30-min, hourly, 4-hour, daily): in an uptrend, bounces into a rising 200 are buy zones; a clean break below the 200 on higher frames flips it to a short signal. The 50 is the secondary structure; the 20 is the near-term momentum read.

**Entry 3 - Moving-average squeeze precedes big moves**
Category: method
When the 50 and 200 SMAs converge (especially when one slopes up and the other slopes down), a large move is building. Price ping-pongs in the tightening range until it breaks free. The squeeze tells you a move is *imminent*, not its direction, so prepare for expansion and let the break confirm direction rather than guessing. Treat converging averages or trendlines (a wedge) the same way: pressure building toward a release.

**Entry 4 - Fibonacci as a structural guardrail (retracements and extensions)**
Category: method
Draw Fibonacci both peak-to-base and base-to-peak, anchored to a meaningful candle (the dominant/highest-volume bar, the opening candle, or a major swing), across 30-min, hourly, and daily. Use retracements to mark where pullbacks should floor (the 50% midpoint is the key bounce zone; 25%/75% are secondary) and extensions to project upside targets beyond the moving averages. Fibs are guardrails and target confirmation used alongside moving averages and structural levels, never in isolation. Note where Fib levels coincide with a pivot or an average: confluence strengthens the level.

**Entry 5 - Gamma walls, the gamma flip, and dealer-hedging mechanics**
Category: method
Read options-derived levels as structure. Call walls sit above price and act as resistance; put walls sit below and act as support; the gamma flip is where the market switches from positive to negative gamma and tends to reject. These walls move as dealer inventory shifts, so track *how* they migrate to gauge flow intent. Zero-DTE walls are short-term intraday references that rotate quickly (can shift within 15 minutes) and should be weighted less than larger-expiry walls. Mechanics matter: if price breaks a call wall and does *not* immediately fade, dealers are forced to hedge by buying, which can extend the move 20-30 points into the next supply zone. Repeated breaches over two or three days signal the wall is losing its hold. This explains *why* her level-touch dips work: dealers are defending gamma.

**Entry 6 - Anchored VWAP, value area, and volume profile**
Category: method
Anchor VWAP to a specific high-volume event (session open, the 9:30 RTH candle, a prior-day open, a dominant volume bar) rather than using a generic VWAP: the precise anchor gives a sharper mean-reversion target and a cleaner read of shallow vs deep dips. A rising VWAP is bullish, flat or downward-sloping VWAP is bearish (sell bounces off it). From market profile, the value area high/low frame where most volume traded: price tends to rotate back into the value area, and losing the value area low signals a deeper pullback or reversal. Unfilled gaps tend to fill eventually, which can serve as a longer-term target.

**Entry 7 - Momentum divergence as a trend-fatigue flag**
Category: method
Watch momentum as a separate signal from price. When price makes a higher high but momentum makes a lower high (or a lower low in price with a higher low in momentum), the move is fatiguing and a reversal at the next structural level becomes likely. The slope (steepness) of the momentum line tells you how committed buyers or sellers are. A common combination she trades: rising price and rising 200 SMA but downward momentum = expect a shallow drift back into support, not a reversal. Use divergence to avoid entering into deteriorating conditions, not as a standalone trigger.

**Entry 8 - Dominant candle and measured moves**
Category: method
Identify the dominant candle (usually the prior-day 9:30 open or the highest-volume bar on the chart): it signals primary institutional order-flow direction. Measure its high-to-low and project that measured move (with Fibonacci ratios) to define target zones and support/resistance for the session. Whether that candle has been breached sets the day's narrative. Bull/bear flag "pole" breaks project a measured continuation. Anchor your levels to this candle and re-anchor when a new dominant candle prints.

**Entry 9 - Trading sideways ranges and boxes**
Category: method
When the market is range-bound (50 flat, pivots clustered), draw a box around the consolidation: buy the low edge, sell the high edge, and do not trade the middle ("buy on the edges of the noise, not in the noise"). Go support-to-resistance or resistance-to-support as a full rotation: do not take partial profits and trail stops up in a range, because that gives back the move. A push above the box that fails ("look above and fail," "shaking the trees") flushes weak hands and is a reversal/continuation tell, not a breakout. Repeated failed shakes at one edge signal the range is about to resolve the other way.

**Entry 10 - Liquidity sweeps and "shaking the trees"**
Category: glossary
Markets deliberately run stops past an obvious low or high to flush weak hands before the real move ("shaking the trees," a liquidity sweep). A break of a low that is quickly recaptured is usually an orchestrated flush, not a trend change, and often leads to mean reversion back up intraday. Recognize it and do not panic-sell into it. This pairs with "look below and fail" (a bullish reversal after a failed lower low) and "look above and fail" (a bearish reversal after a failed higher high).

**Entry 11 - Wait for the fade; never buy the breakout**
Category: psychology
Do not chase breakouts. When price is extended far from the moving averages or sitting right at a first target, the risk/reward is poor: wait for price to fade back toward support (VWAP, pivot, or a demand zone) and enter there, even if waiting takes all day. Dips in an uptrend can be deep and frightening ("savage dips"), and that is precisely where the entry is: trust the structure, not the move. Most traders get whipsawed because they buy the initial break instead of the pullback. If a dip-buy setup fails and stops you out, re-entering is fine when the bigger picture still says dips are buys: one loss does not change the thesis.

**Entry 12 - Get risk small; everything is a shade of gray**
Category: psychology
The goal is to make risk as small as possible, not to be 100% certain of direction. Markets do not offer black-and-white setups; if you wait for perfection you will never trade. In conflicting or "messy" zones, shrink size dramatically (use micros instead of full contracts when close to a target or far from your reference), or step aside entirely: a zero-trade day or a micro day is a win, not a loss. Stay directionally aligned with your trend grade rather than flipping between long and short, which gets messy fast. Keep the toolkit simple (200/50/20, Fibonacci, volume, momentum, gamma walls): do not clutter the chart.

**Entry 13 - Overnight inventory and volume anomalies as open-read inputs**
Category: method
Use the pre-RTH session to scout, not to hold. Read overnight/pre-market inventory: net-long overnight inventory makes a morning dip/unwind more likely, net-short makes a morning bounce more likely. A sudden, unusual burst of pre-market volume (e.g., a 4 a.m. spike) signals news and stress are coming: be ready for a large move and size carefully. The 4-hour chart is useful here to frame the dominant candle, volume accumulation, and overnight intent before the open. Overnight is scouting for the open, not a license to carry positions.

**Entry 14 - Countertrend trade selection (when you must fight the macro)**
Category: method
When you want a bearish trade inside a bullish macro (or vice versa), do not fight the strongest names. Go to the weakest sector, then the weakest name within that sector (a scanner like Benzinga Pro helps). Follow weakness when shorting and strength when buying: never force your favorite name until it has proven the move. Macro frames your bias; it never overrides the structure on the chart.
