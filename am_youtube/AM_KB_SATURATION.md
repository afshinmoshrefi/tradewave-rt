**15. Order-Flow Primacy (the root cause)**
*Category: method.*
The candle and the level are effects, not causes. Order flow is the answer to "which way." Price goes where the volume is. Read levels as places where flow is likely to react, but never trust a level without the flow confirming it. This is the mental layer that sits underneath every other entry.

**16. Wall-Bounded Oscillation and Intraday Wall Migration**
*Category: method.*
Dealers trap price between the put wall and the call wall and defend both. Price ping-pongs inside until it breaks a wall, then accelerates as dealers hedge. Critically, the walls themselves move intraday (zero-DTE walls especially can jump) as dealers re-hedge delta. Re-read the walls during the session, do not treat the morning map as fixed.

**17. Confluence and Vertical-Stack Compression**
*Category: method.*
When pivot, VWAP, anchored VWAP, and the 50 SMA cluster at the same price, that zone is ultra-sticky and high-probability. When the 200, 50, and VWAP stack vertically (landlocked), the market is coiled and a breakaway is near in the direction of the prior trend. Confluence raises conviction; a vertical stack is a timing tell.

**18. Slope and Flat-MA Chop Filter**
*Category: method.*
Direction of a moving average matters as much as price relative to it. A flat 50, flat 200, or flat VWAP signals chop ahead: do not expect clean continuation. Upslope plus price above equals strong; flat VWAP under a "trend" means consolidation and retest are imminent. Use slope to decide whether to trade trend or stand aside.

**19. Volume-Profile Structure: POC, Bimodal Nodes, and Thin Pockets**
*Category: method.*
Point of control is the market's hub and a magnet. Bimodal/multimodal profiles (two or three distinct volume pockets) signal extended chop and mark precise re-test/bounce zones. Thin volume pockets are run through fast once momentum engages, so they forecast the next leg's target. Value-area width reads as inventory: wide equals distribution, narrow equals accumulation.

**20. Session Time-Anchors: 4am London, Midnight, Globex, MOO/MOC**
*Category: method.*
Specific candles are structural reference frames: the 4am London candle sets the daily go/no-go (close above prior pivot favors a trend day), the Globex open is front-line support/resistance, and market-on-open / market-on-close candles anchor the day. The Wednesday midnight candle (per Edgeful) has ~70% odds of being revisited intraday, useful as an NQ threshold. Mark these before the open.

**21. Cumulative Delta and Pre-Market Positioning**
*Category: method.*
Cumulative delta flipping negative pre-market reveals seller dominance before the open, which tells you whether to fade or buy the first move. Read delta as a confirmation layer on top of overnight inventory and the wall map.

**22. Max Pain as a Magnet in Compressed Markets**
*Category: method.*
In sideways, gamma-pinned markets, options max pain acts as a gravitational attractor distinct from ordinary support/resistance. On Friday expiration expect up to three pulls toward it (before open, at open, at close). Use it as the center of gravity, not as a tradeable edge by itself.

**23. Futures Curve: Backwardation, Contango, and Contract Rolls**
*Category: method.*
The forward curve is a structural driver independent of spot. Backwardation lifts prices via roll cost; contango pressures them. Contract rolls create visible volume cliffs, jagged formations, and gap-fills that must complete before continuation (e.g., ES Dec-to-Mar). Treat roll days as mechanical, not directional.

**24. The "If-And-Then" Conditional Entry Rule**
*Category: method.*
Never anticipate; confirm. A valid entry requires two parts: the breakout AND a successful pullback that holds the broken level. If both, then trade; if the level cannot be recaptured, stay out. The corollary: a failed breakdown that recaptures the level confirms reversal up, while a bounce that cannot recapture confirms continuation down. This is her single most repeated risk-structuring rule after the core 14.

**25. Reflexive (Mechanical) Bounces in Hedged/Down Markets**
*Category: psychology/method.*
In a heavily hedged market, bounces after hard selling are automatic and mechanical, not trend reversals. Do not confuse a reflex bounce for a bottom, and do not add shorts into the aggressive fade. The structure of the bounce tells you the next move: look-above-and-go means continuation, look-below-and-fail means sell the bounce. Avoid the falling knife; wait for the reflex to confirm.

**26. Crosscurrent / Multi-Timeframe Disagreement = Battleground**
*Category: psychology.*
When timeframes disagree on trend, it is a battleground that demands tighter risk and smaller size, or standing aside. "Trend is up, momentum is down" is the canonical crosscurrent. Recognize the disagreement explicitly rather than forcing a side.

**27. Seasonality and Calendar Flow Effects**
*Category: faq.*
Repeatable calendar rhythms shape odds without predicting news: Santa rally and post-Christmas drift, January fear, VIX expiring after the indices (creating post-holiday flow flips), buyback-window openings, month-end and quad-witching/OPEX hedging. Use these to game probabilities, but manage daily risk inside them: seasonality is never a license for loose stops.

**28. Confused Option Positioning (Wall Inversions) = Guaranteed Volatility**
*Category: method.*
When the call wall sits below the put wall (or zero-DTE and month-end walls point opposite directions), option traders are structurally offside and will be forced to re-hedge. This positioning tell reliably precedes outsized swings as one side gets flushed out. When walls are in sync, expect consensus and calmer range; when inverted, expect a squeeze.
