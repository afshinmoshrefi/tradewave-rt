Do not chase breakouts. When price is extended far from the moving averages or sitting right at a first target, the risk/reward is poor: wait for price to fade back toward support (VWAP, pivot, or a demand zone) and enter there, even if waiting takes all day.

Dips in an uptrend can be deep and frightening ("savage dips"), and that is precisely where the entry is: trust the structure, not the move. Most traders get whipsawed because they buy the initial break instead of the pullback. If a dip-buy setup fails and stops you out, re-entering is fine when the bigger picture still says dips are buys: one loss does not change the thesis.
<!--PLAIN-->
Picture a shoe sale. The moment the doors open and everyone rushes in, prices are at their highest. If you wait for the crowd to thin out and the excitement to settle, you often get a better deal on the same shoes. That is the whole idea here.

In trading, a "breakout" is when price suddenly surges higher, like those doors flying open. It looks exciting, but at that moment price is often stretched far from the moving averages - the smooth, rolling average lines that show where price has been spending most of its time - and it may already be sitting right at a first target level, a natural resting spot where sellers tend to push back.

When price is that extended, the risk/reward is poor. That means you would be risking a lot to gain a little.

So instead of chasing, Anne-Marie's rule is to "wait for the fade" - even if waiting takes all day. The fade is when price pulls back toward a support area. Support areas she watches include VWAP (the average price weighted by volume traded so far that day), a pivot (a calculated turning-point level), or a demand zone (a price area where buyers have stepped in before). Those pullbacks can feel scary, and she calls the deep ones "savage dips" - but that discomfort is precisely where the better entry sits. Trust the structure, not the move.

Most traders get shaken out because they buy the initial breakout instead of waiting for the pullback. If you enter on a dip and the trade stops you out, re-entering is completely reasonable - as long as the bigger picture still says dips are buys. One loss does not change the thesis when the structure still holds.
