The goal is to make risk as small as possible, not to be 100% certain of direction. Markets do not offer black-and-white setups; if you wait for perfection you will never trade. In conflicting or "messy" zones, shrink size dramatically (use micros instead of full contracts when close to a target or far from your reference), or step aside entirely: a zero-trade day or a micro day is a win, not a loss.

Stay directionally aligned with your trend grade rather than flipping between long and short, which gets messy fast. Keep the toolkit simple (200/50/20, Fibonacci, volume, momentum, gamma walls): do not clutter the chart.
<!--PLAIN-->
Here is the one thing this lesson is really saying: markets are never black and white, so your job is not to be certain - your job is to make losing as small as possible when you are wrong.

Think of it like driving in fog. You do not speed up until you see every inch of road ahead. You slow down, you stay in your lane, and if the fog gets too thick you pull over and wait. Trading in a messy, conflicting zone is that thick fog.

Anne-Marie's rule: when you are close to a price target or far away from your reference point (the level you used to place your trade), shrink your size dramatically. If you normally trade a full futures contract (a standard unit of risk on things like the S&P 500), you drop down to a micro contract, which is one-tenth the size, so a bad trade hurts one-tenth as much. Or you simply sit out. A zero-trade day or a micro day is a win, not a loss. The buffet is open tomorrow.

She also keeps it simple. She uses a short list of tools - moving averages at the 200, 50, and 20-day marks (lines that show the average price over those periods), Fibonacci levels (price zones calculated from a stock's swings), volume clues, momentum readings, and gamma walls (key price levels where options activity tends to act like a magnet or a wall) - and she does not clutter the chart with more.

And she stays on one side at a time, aligned with the overall trend direction, rather than flipping back and forth between buying and selling, which gets messy fast.
