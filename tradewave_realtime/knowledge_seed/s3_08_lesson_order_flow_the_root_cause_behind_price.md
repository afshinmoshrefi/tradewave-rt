Read levels as places where flow is likely to react, but never trust a level without the flow confirming it. This mental layer sits underneath every other entry: dealers, retail, options traders, and institutions all move price through their orders. <!--PLAIN-->
When you look at a candlestick chart (a chart where each bar shows you the open, high, low, and close price for a given time period), it is tempting to think the candle itself is telling you what to do. Anne-Marie wants you to flip that idea around: the candle is just a footprint left behind after something already happened. The real thing that happened was orders - actual buy and sell orders placed by real participants: dealers, retail traders, options traders, and large institutions.

Order flow is simply the stream of those buy and sell orders moving through the market at any moment. Price moves because someone is placing orders large enough to push it. That is the root cause. The candle you see on your screen is the effect, like the dent left in grass after a crowd walks through. The crowd came first.

Volume (the number of contracts or shares actually traded) is your clearest window into that crowd. Price goes where the volume is. Watch for places where volume clusters and where participants bunch up - that is where price gravitates.

Levels (specific price areas you have marked on your chart as meaningful) are places where order flow is likely to show up and react - but likely is not certain. A level on its own is just a hunch. Order flow confirming the level is what turns the hunch into something worth watching. And if the flow is not behind the level, skip it entirely - do not force a trade.

Think of it this way: you can see a crosswalk painted on the road, but that does not mean traffic will stop. You watch the cars slow down and actually stop before you step out.

The practice here is patience - process over P and L. See the level, see where the volume is clustering, and wait for the flow to confirm before you act. No confirmation, no trade.
