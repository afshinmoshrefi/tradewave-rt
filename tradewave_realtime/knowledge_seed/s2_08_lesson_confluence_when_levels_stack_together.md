Imagine you are driving across town and you need to decide where to stop for lunch. If one friend says a diner is good, okay, maybe. But if your GPS, three friends, and a food critic all point to the same spot independently - now you are pretty confident that is the right stop. That is confluence: multiple independent signals agreeing on one price.

In the market, we watch a handful of guideposts. The pivot is a calculated reference price from the previous session. VWAP (Volume-Weighted Average Price) is the average price every share traded at during the day, weighted by how much volume traded there - think of it as where the "average buyer" is sitting. Anchored VWAP is the same idea but anchored to a meaningful starting date instead of today. The 50 SMA and 200 SMA are simple moving averages - they just average the closing price over the last 50 or 200 days and draw a line, showing the general direction price has been traveling.

There are two distinct patterns worth knowing here.

The first is a cluster: when the pivot, VWAP, anchored VWAP, and the 50 SMA all land on or very near the same price, that zone becomes "ultra-sticky." So many different groups of traders are watching it at once that price tends to react strongly there. Confluence raises your conviction in a zone.

The second is a vertical stack: when the 200 SMA, the 50 SMA, and VWAP pile tightly on top of each other and price is landlocked between them - boxed in with nowhere easy to go - the market is coiled like a spring. Anne-Marie calls that a "timing tell": a breakaway move is near, likely in the direction price was already heading before it got pinched.

More levels agreeing on one price means a stronger expected reaction. That is the whole idea: lean harder when all roads lead to one price.
