Dealers trap price between the put wall and the call wall and defend both. Price ping-pongs inside until it breaks a wall, then accelerates as dealers hedge. Critically, the walls themselves move intraday (zero-DTE walls especially) as dealers re-hedge delta. Re-read the walls during the session: do not treat the morning map as fixed. A wall that was here at 9:30 may have jumped by 11:00. The oscillation inside the walls is mechanical, the breakout is the turn. Mark the walls fresh through the day.
<!--PLAIN-->
Picture a game of pinball. The bumpers on the left and right keep the ball bouncing back and forth in a predictable zone - until the ball finally escapes, and then it shoots off fast.

Options dealers create something very similar in the market. A "put wall" is a price level where dealers have sold a large number of put options (contracts that pay out if price falls). A "call wall" is the same idea on the upside. Dealers are required to buy and sell shares to protect themselves as price moves near these levels - a process called "hedging" - and that buying and selling pushes price back toward the middle. The result is that price tends to bounce back and forth, ping-pong style, between those two walls.

The key rule for today: the walls are not fixed. Especially on zero-DTE options (contracts that expire the same day), dealers are constantly adjusting their hedges as the session goes on. A wall that sat at one price level at 9:30 AM can shift to a completely different level by 11:00 AM.

That means the map you drew in the morning is not the map you trade at noon. Re-read the walls during the session and update your marks.

The bounce inside the walls is mechanical and somewhat predictable. A true break through a wall is the real signal, because that forces dealers to hedge in the new direction fast, which adds fuel to the move.

Process over P and L: mark, watch, update, repeat.
