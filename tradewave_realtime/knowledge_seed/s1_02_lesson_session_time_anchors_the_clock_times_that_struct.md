Think of the trading day like a restaurant with a very specific schedule. There is a breakfast crew, a lunch rush, and a dinner shift - and the handoffs between them leave marks on the price chart you can actually see and use.

Anne-Marie teaches that four specific candles (a single price bar on a chart, each one covering a set period of time) act as the skeleton of the day. They are not random. They are the moments when large pools of buyers and sellers are clocking in or out, and price tends to return to those spots.

Here is what each one does:

The 4am London candle: where European trading kicks off. If price closes above the level it was at the prior turning point (called a pivot), that is an early sign the day may trend in one direction rather than chop back and forth.

The Globex open: this is the overnight electronic session open. It acts as a front-line reference point - prices often find support (a floor) or resistance (a ceiling) there.

The market-on-open and market-on-close candles: the official start and official end of the regular session. Big, passive funds are entering or exiting at these moments, so those price levels carry real weight all day.

The midnight candle: a quiet reference zone that price often comes back to touch at some point during the day.

The habit is simple: mark these four before the open, every day. They are not decoration. Process over P and L - knowing where the skeleton is costs you nothing and skips nothing.
