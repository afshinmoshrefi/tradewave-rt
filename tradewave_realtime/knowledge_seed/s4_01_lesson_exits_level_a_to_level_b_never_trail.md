This is where most traders give back their edge, so be clear: **Anne-Marie does not trail
stops.** *"I don't trail any stops. I go level A to level B and I'm done."* And bluntly:
*"trailing stops are terrible."*

The doctrine:

- You enter at a level (A). Your target is the **next structural level** in the direction of
  the trade (B). When price reaches B, you're done - or you reassess for the next leg.
- The first target is often the next level or VWAP; take half there.
- A runner can aim for a measured extension when the trend is strong, but you're always
  exiting *into* structure, not chasing a moving stop that shakes you out of good trades.

Your **stop** is defined by structure too: roughly the width of the entry-trigger candle,
placed at its far edge. If the trigger candle sits inside a much bigger candle (like the
3:30 or 9:30), use the larger candle's width. On a very wide entry candle, take half size at
the break and half at the 50% midpoint, then move your stop to that midpoint to halve risk.

The mindset: **decide A and B before you enter.** Once you're in, the trade is already
planned - you're not improvising with a trailing stop and your emotions. Scalping is the
opposite game ("scalpers revert to zero"); that's not what this is.

Here is the one idea this lesson is built on: before you enter a trade, you pick two spots on the chart - a starting point (call it level A) and a destination (call it level B). When price reaches B, you are done. That is the whole plan.

Anne-Marie puts it plainly: "I don't trail any stops. I go level A to level B and I'm done." A trailing stop is a stop-loss (a price where you exit to limit damage) that moves along as price moves in your favor. It sounds smart, but she says trailing stops are terrible - they shake you out of perfectly good trades with emotion, not structure.

What is "structure"? Think of a highway with rest stops. Certain price levels act like those rest stops - places where the market has stalled, reversed, or bunched up before. Level B is the NEXT natural rest stop in the direction your trade is going. VWAP (a running average of price weighted by volume, a common reference point) often serves as that first target. Take half your position off there, then let the rest aim at the next level beyond.

Your stop (the exit that protects you if you are wrong) is sized by the candle - that is, the price bar - that triggered your entry. Use its far edge as your stop. If that candle is unusually wide, take half your size at the breakout point and the other half at its midpoint, then move your stop to that midpoint so your risk is cut in half.

The key habit: decide A and B before you click. Process over P and L - once you are in, you already have a plan and you are not improvising with your emotions.
