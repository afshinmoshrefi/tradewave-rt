The phrase "futures contract" sounds intimidating. It really isn't, so let me strip it down.

A **futures contract** is a standardized agreement to buy or sell something at a later date. "Standardized" just means everybody's contract is the same size and terms, so they're easy to trade. Long ago these were about real goods, like a farmer locking in a price for corn. Today, most traders never touch the actual thing. We use these contracts to **speculate**, which is a fancy word for trying to profit from price moving up or down. That's it.

What do we trade? Mostly two big ones that act like "the market":

- The **S&P 500** (a basket of 500 large U.S. companies).
- The **Nasdaq** (heavy on technology companies).

When people say "the market is up today," they usually mean one of these moved up.

Now, two words you'll hear constantly: point and tick.

A **point** is one full unit of price movement. If price goes from 5000 to 5001, that's one point.

A **tick** is the smallest step price can take, smaller than a point. Think of a point like a dollar and a tick like a few pennies of that dollar.

Here's the part that matters: **each point of movement is worth a fixed dollar amount**, set by the contract. So when price moves, your account moves by a known amount per point. This is exactly why we go slow and start tiny, which is our next lesson.

You do not need to memorize the dollar figures today. Just hold onto the picture: a futures contract is a simple agreement, and we use it to bet on price moving. Nothing scary about that.
