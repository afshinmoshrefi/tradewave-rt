Markets deliberately run stops past an obvious low or high to flush weak hands before the real move - "shaking the trees," a liquidity sweep. A break of a low that is quickly recaptured is usually an orchestrated flush, not a trend change, and often leads to mean reversion back up intraday.

Recognize it and do not panic-sell into it. This pairs with "look below and fail" (a bullish reversal after a failed lower low) and "look above and fail" (a bearish reversal after a failed higher high). The sweep targets weak-hand stops, then price rotates back to the structural level (demand or support zone) where the real move continues.
<!--PLAIN-->
Picture an apple tree in an orchard. Somebody grabs the trunk and gives it a hard shake - apples fall, the weak ones drop first, and then the tree is calm and ready to produce again. Markets do the exact same thing.

A "liquidity sweep" is when price briefly breaks past a well-known high or low on your chart, just far enough to trigger all the stop-loss orders (automatic "get me out" orders that traders place to protect themselves) that have been stacking up at that level. Those stops represent nervous, or "weak-hand," traders who bail out at the first sign of trouble. Once those orders are filled, the professional money has all the cheap supply or demand it needed, and price quickly snaps back - not just randomly, but to the structural level (a demand or support zone) where the real move begins.

Anne-Marie calls this "shaking the trees." The key word is "quickly." A real trend break keeps going. A sweep reverses fast and recaptures the level it just punctured.

She names two patterns to recognize: "look below and fail" - price dips under a recent low, then closes back above it, which tilts the situation toward a move up. "Look above and fail" - price pokes above a recent high, then closes back under it, which tilts toward a move down.

Your job is not to panic-sell or panic-buy into the shake. Wait, watch, and let the fake-out reveal itself. Process over P and L.
