When you want a bearish trade inside a bullish macro (or vice versa), do not fight the strongest names. Go to the weakest sector, then the weakest name within that sector. Follow weakness when shorting and strength when buying: never force your favorite name until it has proven the move.

Macro frames your bias; it never overrides the structure on the chart. A scanner helps identify the laggards, but the chart - moving averages, Fibonacci, volume - makes the entry decision. The weakest name has the least institutional support and is most likely to break cleanly in a countertrend move.
<!--PLAIN-->
When the overall market is rising but you think something is about to fall, it can be tempting to "short" - bet that the price drops - on a big, flashy name everyone loves. Anne-Marie says: don't. That stock has the most big-money players supporting it, which makes it the hardest to push down. Instead, go to the weakest sector first.

A sector is just a neighborhood of related businesses - think "tech stocks" or "bank stocks." Inside a rising market, one neighborhood is almost always lagging behind. Find that lagging neighborhood, then find the weakest individual stock within it. That stock has the least institutional support - meaning the big funds aren't propping it up as much - so if the market does wobble, it's the most likely to fall cleanly.

The same logic runs in reverse. When you want to buy into a falling market, go to the strongest sector and the strongest name inside it.

Here's the key guardrail: the big-picture direction (macro) only shapes your lean - it never overrides what the chart itself is showing. A scanning tool can help you spot the laggards quickly, but the final entry decision always comes from the chart: moving averages (a running average of recent prices), Fibonacci levels (common reversal zones), and volume (how many shares are trading). Never force a trade on your favorite name until it has actually proven the move.
