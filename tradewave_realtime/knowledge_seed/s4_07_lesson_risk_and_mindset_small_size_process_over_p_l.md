Every trade is a test of structure, not a test of you. Keep the frame wide enough to think clearly.

**Size small first:** Trade micros before minis, and one mini before three. If you cannot clearly read the bias, if timeframes are crossed (trend up, momentum down), or if you are near a major target already, shrink size or sit out. A zero-trade day wins. A micro day that respects process wins. A big account loss because you outsized confidence does not win, ever. Get risk small and stay small until the structure clarity improves.

**Process over money:** Make money without losing money. Defense first. Track whether you followed your process (did you wait for the fade, did you check the confluence, did you size for the bias?), not whether you won the trade. A stopped-out dip-buy that honored the structure is a win. A lucky scratch on an extended chase is a loss that feels like luck. Over fifty trades, process wins. Over five trades, luck wins. Play the long game.

**Reflexive bounces and crosscurrents:** In a heavily hedged market (heavy overnight long inventory, major wall just broken), bounces are mechanical, not reversals. Do not add shorts into an aggressive fade thinking you caught the turn. Wait for the reflex to fade and confirm the direction via look-above-and-fail or look-below-and-fail. When the 30-minute is up but the 4-hour is neutral, you are in a crosscurrent: shrink, do not size into conflict.

**Shaking the trees:** Stops are run. Walls are tested and breached then recaptured daily. A break of a level that is quickly retaken usually means weak hands were flushed (shaking the trees) and the real move is next. Do not panic-sell into the shake. Let the structure breathe and recapture confirm which way the market intends.

**Keep it simple:** Two moving averages (200 and 50), Fibonacci retracements, volume profile, a momentum oscillator. That is the whole toolkit. Do not clutter the chart chasing every indicator. The simpler the read, the faster you spot the setup and the easier you size accordingly.

STAGE 4 - Managing the Trade: Risk, Mindset, and Keeping It Small

Here is the one idea Anne-Marie comes back to again and again: a small, disciplined trade beats a big, messy one every single time.

Start with size. "Micros" are tiny versions of futures contracts - they cost far less per tick than standard "minis." Trade micros before minis, and one mini before three. Why? Because when your position is small, you can think straight. When it is too big, fear takes the wheel.

If you cannot clearly see which way the market is leaning - or if different timeframes are pointing in opposite directions, like the 30-minute chart trending up while the 4-hour chart sits flat and directionless - that is called a "crosscurrent." In a crosscurrent: shrink your size or sit on your hands. A zero-trade day is a win. A micro day that respects the process is a win. Oversizing into a muddled picture is never a win.

The first rule of making money is: do not lose it. Defense comes first. Track your process, not your profit. Ask yourself: did I wait for the fade (meaning, did I let the price pull back before entering)? Did I check that multiple things agreed - trend, momentum, price zone? A trade that honored the rules but got stopped out is still a win. A lucky scratch on a bad, extended entry is still a bad entry, even if it feels like luck. Over five trades, luck can win. Over fifty trades, process always wins. Play the long game.

Know about "reflexive bounces." In a market that has been under heavy pressure overnight and just broke a major level, a quick bounce up is often just mechanics - not a true reversal. Do not jump in adding to a short position thinking you caught the turn. Instead, wait for that bounce to fade, and then watch for what Anne-Marie calls a "look-above-and-fail" or "look-below-and-fail" - the price pokes past a key level, fails to hold, and snaps back. That confirmation tells you which way the market actually intends to go.

Also watch out for "shaking the trees." A key price level breaks briefly and then snaps back. That is usually weak traders getting flushed out, not a real reversal. Do not panic and sell into the shake. Let the price settle and reclaim the level before you decide what it means.

And keep the chart clean: two moving averages (200 and 50), Fibonacci retracements (natural pullback zones), volume profile, and one momentum oscillator. That is the whole kit. More indicators do not mean better reads - they just create noise. The simpler the chart, the faster you spot the setup.
