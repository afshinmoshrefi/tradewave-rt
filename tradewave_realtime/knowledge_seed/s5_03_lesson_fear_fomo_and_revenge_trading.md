Most damage to an account isn't a bad strategy - it's a good strategy abandoned under
emotion. Anne-Marie coaches the three big ones directly:

**Fear** keeps you out of good, rule-based trades or shakes you out early. The fix is a
*pre-planned* trade: when your stop and target are defined before you enter, there's nothing
left to be afraid of in the moment - you already know your worst case and you sized for it.

**FOMO** makes you chase. The fix is patience and the buffet mantra: a missed trade costs
you nothing; a chased trade costs you real money. You can't lose what you never risked.

**Revenge trading** - trying to "make it back" right after a loss - is the account killer.
The fix is mechanical: **after two losers, you stop.** Not "one more to get even." The market
does not know or care that you're down, and trading angry guarantees you compound it.

Her read on a panicking trader: *"I can feel your anxiety across the screen."* When you feel
that anxiety, it's information - it means you're out of process and into emotion. Step back,
breathe, and return to the checklist: What does the trend gate say? Is there a real level?
Is this a planned trade or a reaction? If it's a reaction, you don't take it.

Here is something nobody tells you when you start: most trading accounts do not blow up because of a bad strategy. They blow up because a good strategy gets thrown out the window the moment feelings take over. Anne-Marie identifies three emotions that do most of the damage, and she has a concrete fix for each one.

Fear - the kind that keeps you frozen, or makes you exit a perfectly good trade too early - comes from not knowing your worst case ahead of time. The fix is to plan the trade before you place it. When you decide your stop (the price where you admit you are wrong and get out), your target (where you plan to take profit), and how much of your account you are risking, all before you enter, there is nothing left to figure out in the moment. The scary unknown is already handled.

FOMO - the "fear of missing out" - makes you jump into a trade just because it is already moving, instead of waiting for the right setup. The buffet is open tomorrow. A trade you missed costs you nothing. A trade you chased can cost you real money.

Revenge trading means going right back in after a loss to "make it back." This is the account killer. Anne-Marie's rule is mechanical and non-negotiable: after two losers, you stop. The market does not know you are down, and trading angry just compounds the damage.

When you feel that anxiety rising, treat it as information - it means you have left your process and slipped into emotion. Step back and breathe. Then run through the checklist: What is the trend doing right now? Is there a clear price level driving this move? Is this a planned trade or just a reaction? If the answer to that last question is "reaction," you do not take it. Process over P and L.
