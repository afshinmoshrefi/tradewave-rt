Imagine a rubber band. Stretch it far enough and it snaps back to center. Price on a chart behaves the same way, and the 200 SMA helps you see where "center" is.

SMA stands for Simple Moving Average. It is just the average closing price over the last 200 candles, drawn as a smooth line on the chart. The 200 SMA is the most statistically significant line you will ever use - Anne-Marie calls it the long-term filter and a mean-reversion target. Mean reversion simply means "prices tend to drift back toward their average after wandering too far away."

Here is the one core idea: when price floats far above a rising 20, 50, and 200 SMA all at once, it is like a crowd that has rushed too far ahead of the parade. Demand starts to tire, and price tends to rotate back toward those averages. That drift back is the dip-buy setup. Watch for when the three averages themselves start to squeeze together - that tightening is the signal the setup is getting close. When price bounces into a rising 200 SMA on a bigger timeframe - the 30-minute, hourly, 4-hour, or daily chart - those are the buy zones Anne-Marie watches. If price breaks cleanly below the 200 SMA on a higher timeframe, the line that was support can flip to resistance, which shifts the bias short.

The 50 SMA gives you secondary structure (a mid-level reference), and the 20 SMA shows near-term momentum. The 200 is the anchor for all of it.
