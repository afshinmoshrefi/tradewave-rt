In a heavily hedged market, bounces after hard selling are automatic and mechanical, not trend reversals. Do not confuse a reflex bounce for a bottom, and do not add shorts into the aggressive fade. The structure of the bounce tells you the next move: look above and go means continuation, look below and fail means sell the bounce. Avoid the falling knife and wait for the reflex to confirm. Respect that the bounce is coming, then read whether it holds.
<!--PLAIN-->
Picture a rubber ball dropped onto concrete. The moment it hits the floor it bounces - not because it has decided to go up, but because the laws of physics make it spring back automatically. That involuntary bounce is not the ball "reversing direction" and heading for the ceiling. It is just physics.

Markets do the same thing after hard, fast selling. When there has been heavy hedging going on - meaning large institutions are buying protection against their positions falling - the mechanics of that insurance force automatic buying the moment prices drop sharply. The price bounces, but it is a reflex, not a sign that sellers have given up and buyers are taking over.

Anne-Marie calls this the reflexive bounce, and her one rule is: do not confuse a reflex for a bottom.

Two things can happen after the bounce:

First, price "looks above and goes" - it pushes above a recent reference level and holds there. That signals continuation; the bounce may have real legs.

Second, price "looks below and fails" - it pops up, then rolls back under that same level. That is the signal to sell the bounce; sellers are still in charge.

The lesson is patience. Do not short into the aggressive drop (that is catching a falling knife), and do not assume the bounce means the bottom is in. Respect that the bounce is coming, then wait and read what it does. Watch whether it holds, then act on the signal.
