Every session has one candle - usually the one printed right at the 9:30 open the day before, or the bar with the most trading volume on your chart - that shows you which way the big institutions were pushing the market. Anne-Marie calls this the dominant candle. Think of it like the loudest voice in a room: whoever speaks loudest tends to set the mood for everyone else.

Once you find that candle, you measure the distance from its top to its bottom. That distance is your measured move - the market's way of hinting at how far price might travel in the same direction. You then use Fibonacci ratios (fixed percentages that traders have used for decades to estimate where price might pause or reverse) to mark out target zones and support/resistance areas on your chart.

Whether price has stayed inside that candle or broken through it tells you the story of the day. If it breaks out like a flag on a pole breaks free, you project that same pole-length forward to estimate a target zone. If the dominant candle holds firm, the measured move acts like a confidence ceiling - a rough upper limit until something changes.

This is especially worth knowing if you trade NQ (Nasdaq futures): Anne-Marie uses the peak-to-base and base-to-peak Fibonacci measurements from the opening candle to set pullback floors and upside extension targets across the whole session.

When a new dominant candle prints, re-anchor your levels to the new one. Process over P and L: the goal is to see these zones clearly before anything happens, not to chase after the fact.
