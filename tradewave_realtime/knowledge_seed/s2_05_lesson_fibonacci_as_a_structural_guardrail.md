Draw Fibonacci both peak-to-base and base-to-peak, anchored to a meaningful candle (the dominant or highest-volume bar, the opening candle, or a major swing), across the 30-minute, hourly, and daily. Use retracements to mark where pullbacks should floor (the 50 percent midpoint is the key bounce zone, 25 and 75 percent are secondary) and extensions to project upside targets beyond the moving averages. Fibonacci levels are guardrails and target confirmation used alongside moving averages and structural levels, never in isolation. Where a Fib level lines up with a pivot or an average, that confluence strengthens it.
<!--PLAIN-->
Imagine you are watching a ball bounce down a staircase. You want to know ahead of time which steps it is likely to land on before it rolls lower or bounces back up. That is exactly what Fibonacci levels do for a price chart - they mark the most common "landing steps" a market tends to revisit after a big move.

Here is the simple idea. When price makes a significant move - say, from a low point up to a high point - traders divide that distance into percentage slices: 25%, 50%, and 75% of the way back. The 50% level is the most important one. Think of it as the halfway landing. Price pulls back (drops a bit after rising, or rises a bit after falling) and very often pauses or turns right there. The 25% and 75% marks are secondary - they matter, but the midpoint is the one Anne-Marie watches first.

Fibonacci also works in the other direction. Beyond the starting point of the original move, you can project extension levels - these are targets for how far price could travel once it resumes. Think of them as the next flights of stairs above the landing. Anne-Marie uses these extensions to set upside targets that go beyond where the moving averages (smoothed price lines) currently sit.

You draw these levels from the bottom of a move to the top, AND from the top back to the bottom, anchored to a meaningful bar - the biggest-volume candle, the opening candle, or a major swing high or low - on the 30-minute, hourly, and daily charts.

But here is the guardrail: Fibonacci levels are NOT used alone. They only get weight when they line up with another reference point you are already watching - a pivot (a prior swing high or low) or a moving average. When two or more of those agree on the same spot, that agreement - called confluence - makes the level much more reliable.

Process over P and L: mark the levels first, then let the chart come to them. Ask the coach any question, or read Anne-Marie's full version for the complete detail.
