In sideways, gamma-pinned markets, options max pain acts as a gravitational attractor distinct from ordinary support and resistance. On a Friday expiration expect up to three pulls toward it (before the open, at the open, and into the close). Use it as the center of gravity, not as a tradeable edge by itself. <!--PLAIN-->
Some days the market just sits there - flat, quiet, going nowhere. Anne-Marie calls this a "gamma-pinned" market. Here is what that means in plain English.

Options are contracts that give buyers the right to buy or sell a stock or index at a specific price. Every Friday, a batch of those contracts expires - they are done. The price level where the most of those contracts expire worthless (costing buyers the most money and saving the sellers the most money) is called "max pain." Think of it like a drain in the middle of a bathtub: when the water is still, everything drifts toward the drain.

One thing worth knowing: max pain is not the same as a regular support or resistance level on a chart. It comes from options positioning, not from price history. That is what makes it its own category.

On a Friday expiration day, when the market is quiet and not clearly going up or down, Anne-Marie says to expect the price to get pulled toward that max pain level up to three times - once before the market opens, once right at the open, and once heading into the close.

The key rule: treat max pain as the center of gravity, not as a signal to trade. It tells you where price is being pulled. It does not tell you to buy or sell anything.

And here is the other side of the coin: when the market IS trending strongly in one direction, max pain matters far less. The pull of the drain disappears when the water is moving fast.

So the discipline is: notice when things are quiet, respect the pull, and wait. Process over P and L.
