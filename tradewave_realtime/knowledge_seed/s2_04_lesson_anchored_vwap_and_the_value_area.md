A rising VWAP is bullish, a flat or falling VWAP is bearish (sell bounces off it). <!--PLAIN-->
Imagine a grocery store tallying up the average price customers paid for apples all day. If most apples sold between $1.80 and $2.20, that range is where buyers and sellers agreed on "fair value." VWAP - the Volume-Weighted Average Price - does the same thing for a stock or futures contract: it is the average price weighted by how many shares or contracts traded at each price level.

Rather than using a generic VWAP, Anne-Marie anchors it - she pins the starting point to a specific high-volume moment, like the 9:30 open, the prior day's open, or a bar where an unusually large amount of volume traded. That precise anchor gives you a sharper picture of where the "average fair price" sits from that moment forward, and a cleaner read of whether a dip is shallow or deep.

Once you have it, the direction tells you the story. A VWAP that is rising says buyers are in control - price is staying above average. A flat or falling VWAP says sellers are in control, and bounces up to it tend to fade back down.

Pair that with the value area from market profile - the price range where most of that session's volume traded, a zone where most participants agreed to trade. Price tends to drift back into that range after moving away from it. If price drops below the bottom of the value area, expect a deeper pullback, not a quick snap back.

Finally, unfilled gaps - price jumps that left an empty space on the chart - tend to get filled over time and can serve as longer-term targets to keep on your radar.
