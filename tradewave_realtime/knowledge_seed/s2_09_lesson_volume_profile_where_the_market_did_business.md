Point of control is the market's hub and a magnet. Bimodal or multimodal profiles (two or three distinct volume pockets) signal extended chop and mark precise re-test and bounce zones. Thin volume pockets get run through fast once momentum engages, so they forecast the next leg's target. Value-area width reads as inventory: wide equals distribution, narrow equals accumulation. Read the profile to see where the market actually traded, where buyers and sellers met most, and where they will fight again. The POC is not resistance, it is gravity.
<!--PLAIN-->
Think of a farmers market. At the end of the day you notice that most people clustered around the apple booth - that booth did the most business. In trading, the volume profile is a tool that shows you where the most buying and selling actually happened during a session. The price level where the heaviest trading occurred is called the Point of Control, or POC. Just like that apple booth draws people back again and again, the POC acts like gravity - price tends to drift back toward it, not because it is a barrier, but because that is where the most agreements between buyers and sellers were made.

Now, sometimes the crowd split between two booths - apples at one end and berries at another, with a quiet middle. That is a bimodal profile (two busy zones) or multimodal (three or more). When you see this shape, the market is likely to chop - bounce back and forth - between those zones rather than trend cleanly. Those busy zones become your re-test levels to watch.

The quiet areas in between, where almost nobody traded, are called thin volume pockets. Price tends to move through them quickly once it gets going, so they help you see where the next move might reach.

Finally, how wide the busy zone is tells you something: a wide value area suggests the market was distributing - spreading out - while a narrow one suggests accumulation, or gathering.
