Never anticipate, confirm. A valid entry needs two parts: the breakout AND a successful pullback that holds the broken level. If both happen, then you trade. If the level cannot be recaptured, you stay out. The corollary: a failed breakdown that recaptures the level confirms a reversal up, while a bounce that cannot recapture confirms continuation down. Break and hold, then act. This is her most repeated risk-structuring rule after the core: it keeps you out of the fake-out and in the real move.
<!--PLAIN-->
Most beginners see a price level get crossed and they jump in immediately. Anne-Marie calls that anticipating, and it is the fastest way to get shaken out of a trade. The rule she comes back to more than almost any other: confirm before you enter, never jump ahead.

Here is what confirmation looks like. Imagine a fence in a yard. A breakout is when someone pushes through the gate and steps onto the other side. But a fake-out is when they step through and then step right back inside, slamming the gate behind them. You do not want to be the person who ran to greet them on the other side before you knew they were actually staying.

So you wait for two things, not one. First, the price crosses the level (the breakout). Second, the price pulls back, tests that same level, and holds there - meaning the fence that was once a barrier now becomes the floor. Only when BOTH things happen do you have permission to enter. That is the if-and-then: IF it breaks AND IF the pullback holds, THEN you trade.

The same rule works in reverse. If a price drops below a level but then climbs back above it, that failed breakdown is a signal that the move may be turning up. If the bounce cannot reclaim the level, the move is likely continuing down.

Break first, hold second, act third. Process over P and L.
