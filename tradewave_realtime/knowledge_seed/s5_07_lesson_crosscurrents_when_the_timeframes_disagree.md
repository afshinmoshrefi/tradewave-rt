Imagine you're driving to a new neighborhood and your phone's GPS says "turn left" while a road sign says "road closed ahead." Two sources of information are pointing in opposite directions. That uncomfortable moment - where you have to choose without enough clarity - is exactly what a "crosscurrent" feels like in trading.

In trading, we watch the market on different timeframes. A timeframe is simply how much time each bar or candle on your chart represents - a 5-minute chart versus a 30-minute chart, for example. "Trend" is the general direction price has been traveling over a stretch of time. "Momentum" is how much force or energy is behind that move right now.

A crosscurrent happens when those two things tell you different things. The classic example Anne-Marie names here is: trend is up (the longer view shows rising prices) but momentum is down (the shorter view shows prices losing steam or pulling back). They are disagreeing.

Here is the core lesson: a crosscurrent is not a signal to trade harder or pick a side and muscle through it. It is a signal to get risk small - smaller size, tighter stop - or simply stand aside and wait. Name the disagreement out loud. Say it to yourself: "these timeframes do not agree." That honesty alone keeps you from forcing a trade that is not there.

The cleanest, highest-confidence setups come when the timeframes line up and agree. When they do not, the buffet is open tomorrow.
