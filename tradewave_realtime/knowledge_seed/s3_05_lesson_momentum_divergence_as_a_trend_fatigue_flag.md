Watch momentum as a separate signal from price. When price makes a higher high but momentum makes a lower high (or a lower low in price with a higher low in momentum), the move is fatiguing and a reversal at the next structural level becomes likely. The slope of the momentum line shows how committed buyers or sellers are. A common combination: rising price and a rising 200 SMA but falling momentum equals a shallow drift back into support, not a reversal. Use divergence to avoid entering into deteriorating conditions, not as a standalone trigger.
<!--PLAIN-->
Think of a car merging onto the highway. The speedometer (that is momentum - a reading of how fast price is moving, not how far) can tell you something the odometer cannot: is the engine still pressing the gas, or is the driver quietly lifting their foot?

Momentum divergence is when price and that speedometer disagree. If price climbs to a new high but momentum shows a lower reading than it did on the last high, the engine is losing power even though the car is still moving forward. The same thing works in reverse - a new price low with a higher momentum low (sellers are losing their grip even as price dips further) suggests the move is running out of steam.

Anne-Marie pairs this with a simple structural clue. If price is rising AND the 200 SMA (simple moving average, the average of the last 200 closing prices) is also pointing upward - meaning the broad trend is still alive and climbing - but momentum is fading, the most likely outcome is a gentle drift back toward a support level, not a sharp collapse.

The key guard here is everything is a shade of gray: divergence is a warning light, not a green light to act. It tells you conditions are deteriorating so you should hold off on entering a trade, rather than using it as a trigger all by itself.

Process over P and L - using divergence to avoid a bad entry is just as valuable as finding a good one.
