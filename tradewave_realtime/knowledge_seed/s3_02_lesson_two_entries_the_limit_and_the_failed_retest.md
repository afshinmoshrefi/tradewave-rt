A level is only a candidate. Anne-Marie takes it two ways, and **always with limit orders - 
never market orders** (a market order in a thin book gets chewed up).

**Pattern A - the pre-placed limit.** On a clean trend day, you place your limit at the
trend-aligned level *in advance* and let price come to you. No chasing. Price does the work.

**Pattern B - the failed retest (her default).** *"I rarely take a breakdown trade. What I
will take is a failed retest - a look above and fail, a look below and fail."* Price pokes
through the level to run the obvious stops, then closes back on the original side. That
"breach candle" is the tell. You enter as price reclaims the level, with your stop just
beyond the breach candle's extreme.

The logic of both: you want price *rejecting* a level in your direction, confirmed by the
trend gate - not a hopeful guess that a level will hold. The failed retest is powerful
because it traps the crowd that chased the obvious break, and you're positioned the other
way as they cover.

Confirmation she layers on: VWAP on the right side, momentum agreeing, enough volume, and
the level not being an exhaustion zone (R3/R4 territory = fade, don't chase).

A price level on a chart is just a candidate - it is not a guarantee. Anne-Marie uses two specific ways to enter trades at those levels, and both share one rule she never breaks: use a limit order, not a market order. A limit order says "I will only buy at this exact price or better." A market order says "give me whatever price is available right now," and in a thin, low-activity market that can cost you badly.

Pattern A is the pre-placed limit. On a day where price is clearly trending in one direction, you place your limit order at a meaningful level ahead of time, then walk away. You let price come to you. No chasing.

Pattern B is the failed retest - her preferred entry. Here is the everyday version: imagine a crowd rushes through what they think is an open door, only to find it locked. They all pile back out. That is what happens when price "pokes" through a key level, triggers the obvious cluster of stop orders sitting just beyond it, and then closes back on the other side of that level. That poke-and-return candle is called the "breach candle," and it is the tell. You enter as price comes back across the level, with your stop placed just beyond the far end of that breach candle. The crowd that chased the obvious breakout is now trapped on the wrong side, and you are positioned the other way as they scramble to get out.

The logic is the same in both patterns: you want price rejecting a level in your favor, confirmed by the trend you already identified.

Before she pulls the trigger on either pattern, Anne-Marie runs a quick checklist: VWAP (the volume-weighted average price for the day) should be on the right side of the trade, momentum should agree with the direction, there should be enough volume behind the move, and the level should not be deep in exhaustion territory - if price is already stretched to R3 or R4 (the third or fourth resistance zone above the open), the right move is to fade the chase, not join it.

Remember, process over profit and loss - you are not guessing, you are waiting for evidence.

Ask the coach to walk you through either pattern on a recent chart example.
