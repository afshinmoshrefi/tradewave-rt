Once trend is graded and bias is set, entry is mechanical. No emotion, no early calls. Wait for the setup to mature.

**The conditional:** Never buy a breakout or chase price when it is extended from structure. If the trend says dips are buys, wait for dips. Let price rotate back to anchored VWAP, the pivot, or a Fibonacci retest zone, then enter there. The rule is "if and then": if price breaks a level AND the broken level holds on pullback (the recapture), then the move is real. If the level cannot be recaptured, the move was a fake and you stay flat. This is the single biggest filter against whipsaws.

**Entry placement:** Size into the fade, not the spike. When uptrend, place buys at the 50% Fibonacci retest, the rising 200 SMA, or anchored VWAP. When downtrend, place shorts at the 50% extension up or into a failing bounce at the prior wall. The second prettiest girl (the second attempt at the same level after a failed first attempt) is often better than the initial breakout.

**Confusing zones:** When bias conflicts (timeframes disagree, momentum is down but price is up, gamma walls are inverted) shrink size or stand aside. Everything is a shade of gray, not black or white. A micro trade or a zero-trade day is a win if it keeps risk small and process intact.

**Hold and exit:** Once short, do not exit a bounce without a structural reason. Once long into a dip, hold until the next target (Fibonacci extension, the value area high, or a measured move off the dominant candle). Use momentum divergence as a fatigue flag, not a standalone exit. Let structure manage you, not the urge to lock in quick gains.

Once you know the trend direction and have a read on which side you want to be on, Anne-Marie says entry becomes almost mechanical. No emotion, no jumping the gun. You wait for the setup to finish cooking.

The core rule is "if and then." If price breaks through a key level AND then comes back and holds that same level on a pullback (traders call that a "recapture" - the level that used to be resistance is now acting as support), THEN the move is likely real. If price cannot hold that level when it returns to it, the breakout was probably a head-fake - the kind that cleans out traders who chased too soon. Staying flat there is the win. This one filter alone cuts out most bad trades.

The "fade" means waiting for price to pull back toward you rather than running after it. A pullback is just price retracing a portion of its move before continuing. So in an uptrend, instead of buying after a big green spike, you wait for price to drift back to a natural resting area - like the 50% midpoint of the recent move (a Fibonacci retest), the rising 200-day moving average (a long-term trend line that often acts as a floor), or the anchored VWAP (a volume-weighted average price that acts like a gravity line for the day). You buy the dip to those levels, not the spike away from them. In a downtrend, the same logic flips: you look to get short on a bounce up to the prior resistance wall, not as price is already falling fast.

One more thing she notes: the second attempt at the same level is often better than the first. If price tries to break a level, fails, and then comes back and tries again, that second setup tends to be cleaner and more reliable than chasing the very first move. She calls this "the second prettiest girl."

When signals conflict and nothing is clean - when different timeframes disagree, or momentum and price seem to be saying opposite things - you shrink your involvement or step away entirely. As she says, everything is a shade of gray. A small trade or a zero-trade day is a win if it keeps your risk small and your process intact.

Once you are in a trade, hold it until price reaches the next logical target - the next Fibonacci extension level, the value area high (the upper boundary of where most trading happened that day), or a measured distance calculated off the biggest candle in the move. Do not exit just because price wiggles against you for a moment. Only exit early if there is a structural reason, like momentum showing signs of exhaustion (called a divergence - where price makes a new high but the momentum reading does not). Let the structure of the market manage the trade, not the urge to lock in a quick profit.
