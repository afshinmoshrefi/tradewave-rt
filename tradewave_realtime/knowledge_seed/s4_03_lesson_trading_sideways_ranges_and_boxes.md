When the market is range-bound (50 SMA flat, pivots clustered), draw a box around the consolidation: buy the low edge, sell the high edge, and do not trade the middle. Go support-to-resistance or resistance-to-support as a full rotation: do not take partial profits and trail stops up in a range, because that gives back the move.

A push above the box that fails ("look above and fail," "shaking the trees") flushes weak hands and is a reversal or continuation tell, not a breakout. Repeated failed shakes at one edge signal the range is about to resolve the other way. Trade the edges of the noise, not in the noise.
<!--PLAIN-->
Sometimes the market just stops going anywhere. Prices bounce up, come back down, bounce up again - and keep doing the same thing over and over inside a tight band. Traders call this a "range" or a "box."

How do you spot one? Anne-Marie looks at the 50 SMA - that is a simple moving average, basically a line showing where price has averaged over the last 50 bars. When that line goes flat instead of angling up or down, and the recent turning points (called pivots - the highs and lows where price reversed) are all clustered near each other, you are likely in a range. The move is: draw a box around all that noise, buy near the bottom edge of the box, sell near the top edge, and skip everything in the middle.

The key rule here is to ride the full rotation from one side to the other. Do not take partial profit and then trail your stop up inside the range - you will just give the move back.

Now, sometimes price pokes just above the top of the box and then snaps back in. That is called "look above and fail," or "shaking the trees" - the market briefly fools people into thinking it is breaking out, flushes out nervous traders, and then drops back. That failed shake is information, not a breakout. If you see it happen repeatedly at the same edge, the range is probably about to resolve in the opposite direction.

Trade the edges, not the noise in the middle.
