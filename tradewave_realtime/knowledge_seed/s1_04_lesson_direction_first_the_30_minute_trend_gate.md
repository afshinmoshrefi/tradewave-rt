Before anything else, decide **direction**, and let the 30-minute chart make that call - 
not your opinion, not the news.

- **Long-only** when price is above the 50 SMA, which is above the 200 SMA, and **both are
  sloping up**. You only buy dips.
- **Short-only** on the inverse - price below the 50 below the 200, both sloping down. You
  only sell bounces.
- **Stand down** when the moving averages are flat or tangled. A flat MA means "as much
  behavior under the line as above it" - there's no edge, so you don't force it.

The 200 SMA is the anchor. As Anne-Marie puts it, *"the 200 SMA is the most statistically
significant indicator on the chart."* Its **slope** - today's reading versus yesterday's - 
sets the tone for the whole session and tends to stick.

Why this matters: most blow-ups come from fighting the trend - buying a falling market
because it "looks cheap." The trend gate takes that fight off the table. If the gate says
long, you are hunting *only* for long setups. If it's flat, your best trade is often no
trade. Direction is a permission, not a prediction.

Before you look at a single trade, Anne-Marie wants you to answer one question: which way is the market leaning right now? And she is very clear - you let the chart answer that, not your gut and not the morning headlines.

Here is how you do it. Open the 30-minute chart (each "candle" or bar on that chart covers half an hour of trading). You are going to look at two lines called moving averages - specifically the 50 SMA and the 200 SMA. SMA stands for Simple Moving Average: it is the average price over the last 50 bars, or the last 200 bars, drawn as a smooth line so you can see the trend at a glance.

Of the two, the 200 SMA is the anchor. Anne-Marie calls it "the most statistically significant indicator on the chart." The direction it is pointing - compare where it sits today versus yesterday - sets the tone for the whole session, and that tone tends to hold.

Three situations:

1. Price is above the 50 SMA, the 50 SMA is above the 200 SMA, and both lines are angled upward - that is your green light to look for long trades only (buying, betting the price goes up). You wait for a dip and buy into it.

2. The opposite picture - price below both lines, both lines angled downward - is your green light to look for short trades only (selling, betting the price falls). You wait for a small bounce and sell into it.

3. The lines are flat or crossed and tangled? Stand down. A flat moving average means there is "as much behavior under the line as above it" - no clear edge. When the gate says stand down, your best move is often no trade at all.

Here is why this rule exists: most serious losses in trading come from fighting the trend - buying a falling market because it "looks cheap." The direction gate takes that fight off the table completely. If the gate says long, you hunt only for long setups. If it is flat, zero trades is a perfectly good result.

Think of direction as a permission slip, not a forecast. It does not tell you what the market will do - it tells you which side of the boat you are allowed to fish from today.
