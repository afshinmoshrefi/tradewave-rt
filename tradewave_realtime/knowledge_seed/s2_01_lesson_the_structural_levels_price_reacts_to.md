Anne-Marie doesn't predict price - she waits for price to come to a **level** and reads the
reaction. The levels she watches:

- Prior-day high, low, and close
- Globex high/low and the midnight midpoint
- The 4 AM Europe candle high/low (and close)
- The 9:30–10:00 opening range high/low
- The prior 30-minute high/low
- Woody's pivots (and Camarilla pivots - those work on NQ, not ES)
- The institutional (3:30) candle high/low
- VWAP

One nuance: **VWAP is permission and a target, not an entry destination.** *"You can't just
go, 'hey, I'm buying VWAP'"* - it gets retested all day. Its slope tells you regime (flat =
chop) and which side you should be trading.

When price arrives at a trend-aligned level, that's a **candidate** - not yet a trade. A
level she calls a *"watering hole"* is one price keeps returning to; those are your highest-
probability reaction zones. The skill is patience: mark the levels in advance, then let the
market walk into them.

Anne-Marie does not try to predict where price is going. Instead, she draws specific lines on her chart ahead of time and then waits for price to walk up to one of those lines. She calls them "levels," and the list is shorter than you might think.

Here is what she watches: the prior day's highest price, lowest price, and closing price. The overnight session's high and low, plus the price at midnight (the midpoint of the night session in time). The high, low, and close of the 4 AM candle, when European markets open. The range established in the first thirty minutes after the 9:30 open. The high and low of the prior thirty-minute candle. A set of calculated lines called Woody's pivots (and a variation called Camarilla pivots, which she uses for the NQ contract rather than ES). The 3:30 institutional candle's high and low. And finally VWAP - the volume-weighted average price, which is roughly the "fair price" that accounts for how much trading happened at each level throughout the day.

One nuance she is very clear about: VWAP is permission to trade in a direction and a target to aim for, not a spot to blindly enter a trade. Its angle matters too - a flat, sideways VWAP signals choppy, directionless conditions, while a sloping VWAP tells you which side of the market you should be on.

When price reaches one of these lines and the direction of the trade matches the overall trend, that becomes a candidate - not a trade yet, just a zone worth watching. Levels that price keeps coming back to again and again are what she calls "watering holes" - those are your highest-probability spots.

Mark the levels before the session, then let the market come to you.
