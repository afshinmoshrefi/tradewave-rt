If you have never traded options, the words "gamma wall" sound like something from a sci-fi movie. Let me make this concrete.

When big institutions buy options contracts, the firms on the other side of those trades - called dealers - have to protect themselves from losing money. They do that by buying or selling the underlying stock or index as the price moves. This mechanical buying and selling creates invisible walls that the market tends to bounce off of, like guardrails on a highway.

Here is the language Anne-Marie uses:

- Call wall: a price level above the current market where dealers are likely to push price back down, acting as resistance (a ceiling).
- Put wall: a price level below the current market where dealers tend to prop price up, acting as support (a floor).
- Gamma flip: the specific price level where the market transitions from one dealer-behavior regime to the other. Price near this zone tends to get rejected.

One important detail - these walls are not permanent. As dealers adjust their positions throughout the day, the walls shift. Walls tied to options that expire the same day (zero-DTE) rotate especially fast and carry less weight than walls on longer-dated contracts.

Here is why this matters practically: when price breaks above a call wall and does NOT immediately fall back, dealers are forced to buy in order to hedge, and that buying can push price all the way to the next supply zone above. This is one reason why patient dip-buys along these levels can work.
