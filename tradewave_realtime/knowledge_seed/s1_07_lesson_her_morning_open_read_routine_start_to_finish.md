Before the market opens, establish the day's structural bias with a five-minute routine. This grade runs once and frames whether you hunt dips or sell bounces for the whole session.

**The process:** Pull the 30-minute chart. Read the alignment and slope of the 20, 50, and 200 SMAs. Positive trend (averages rising, stacked bullishly) says dips are buys. Negative trend (averages falling, bears in charge) says bounces are sell zones. Sideways (50 flat, 200 flat, pivots clustered) says trade the edges only, stand aside from the middle.

Next, layer structure: Fibonacci retracements peak-to-base and base-to-peak anchored to the prior dominant candle or opening bar, checking the 50% retest floor and extension targets. Read the overnight inventory (net-long says expect a dip-unwind; net-short says expect a bounce-fade). Mark the gamma walls (call walls above price as resistance, put walls below as support) and note if they are inverted or in sync. Anchored VWAP to the prior-day close or the 9:30 open, rising or flat or falling.

Watch the 4am London candle and the 200 SMA on the 4-hour: they are your macro confirmation that bias is aligned across timeframes.

That's it. Grade complete. Now you trade inside that grade until the trend gate flips.

Before a single trade, Anne-Marie spends about five minutes reading the market like a weather report. That read sets the tone for the whole day - and it does not change unless the market gives her a clear reason to flip it.

Here is what she does. She pulls up a 30-minute chart (each bar on the screen represents 30 minutes of price movement) and looks at three simple moving averages - smoothed-out trend lines at 20, 50, and 200 periods that show whether price has been generally climbing, falling, or going nowhere. If all three are rising and stacked in order, the trend is pointing up, so she looks to buy dips (temporary pullbacks in price). If they are falling, she looks to sell bounces (temporary recoveries). If they are flat and tangled, she trades only the clear outer edges of the range and stays out of the messy middle.

Next she layers in structure. She draws Fibonacci retracements (specific mathematical ratios traders use to spot where price often pauses or reverses) from the high down to the low and from the low up to the high, anchored to the most important recent candle or the opening bar. The 50% level gets special attention - it is the most common spot where price comes back to test before deciding which way to go next. She also checks overnight inventory: if most traders went home holding long positions (bets that price goes up), she expects a dip early in the session as they exit; if most were short (bets that price goes down), she expects a bounce-and-fade. Then she marks the gamma walls - price levels where options dealers are forced to buy or sell heavily to hedge their books. Call walls sitting above current price act as a ceiling; put walls sitting below act as a floor. She notes whether those walls are lined up with the trend or running against it. Finally she plots the anchored VWAP (the average price since a specific starting point, weighted by how much volume traded there - in this case anchored to the prior-day close or the 9:30 open) and checks whether it is rising, flat, or falling.

As a big-picture sanity check she also watches the 4am London candle and the 200-period average on the 4-hour chart to confirm the bias holds across timeframes.

That is the whole morning read. Everything she does for the rest of the session trades inside that framework - until the trend itself flips. Process over P and L.
